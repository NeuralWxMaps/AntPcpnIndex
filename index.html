Areal Antecedent Precipitation Across the United States

Antecedent Precipitation Index(API) has been a tool used by hydrologists for decades as a proxy for how much moisture might be present in soils. Calculating API involves accumulating precipitation in an iterative process after multiplying the previous API value by a certain reduction factor. When the reduction factor is around 0.80, measured precipitation on one day will reduce to zero after about 14 days. Using data collected from the Real Time Mesoscale Analysis(RTMA) dataset, hourly precipitation has been summed up into six-hour periods. These 6-hour precipitation fields are added together iteratively, after multiplying the sum by around 0.95 before adding the next timestep. This corresponds to about a 24 hour reduction rate around 80 percent.

Values in these images are in millimeters, so 25.4 mm equals 1 inch. The color scale is also floating, depending on the maximum value across the domain. Because of this, the wettest region will be plotted in red, but that value can range from less than 35 mm (less than about 1.50 inches) up to more than 200 mm (more than 8 inches). White will always be zero and the dark blue will generally be less than 25 mm (1.00 inch), indicating areas that are experiencing localized drought.

Since evaporation varies over the course of the year, I modify the reduction rate, from 0.98 per 6 hours during the months of December and January down to 0.93 per 6 hours in June and July.

Precipitation for this study, has been collected across the western United States since mid-October 2021. Collection of precipitation across the eastern United States occurred during and after a heavy rain event in July 2022 and during the landfall of major Hurricane Ian in late September 2022. Eastern United States rainfall has now been collected since late March 2025.

The RTMA dataset is primarily derived from precipitation estimates of radar networks deployed across the United States. Biases in the estimated precipitation values are corrected after a few days by precipitation values reported by local rain gage networks. Since areal coverage of the national weather radar network is more complete over the eastern half of the United States, reliable coverage of API across that part of the is more complete. Over the western United States, areas of low API may or may not be due to a lack of precipitation.

Values of API do not change much over the course of six hours, or even from day to day, unless a significant amount of precipitation has recently fallen. Therefore, I only display the API every tenth day, specifically on the 5th, 15th and 25th of each month. API image files are being retained at the daily time scale, and the liquid precipitation data is being retained at 6-hour time resolution, to assist in conducting case studies.

Plotting the data year after year will hopefully begin revealing climatological signals. Areas experiencing high API values are probably dealing with flooding issues. Regions experiencing dry conditions or localized drought become evident as values of API approach zero. Over the past several summers, across the western United States, large and destructive wildfires have tended to occur in areas with the API at or near zero.
